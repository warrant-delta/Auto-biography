Surviving the Unsurvivable

Understanding the Psychological Impact of Severe Childhood Trauma and Chronic Instability

Presented by Marshall Meinema

Preamble - Marshall Meinema

To the Staff of CUPS Calgary:

My name is Marshall Meinema. I am writing to you not as a complaint, but as documentation of systemic failure that has persisted for years despite my repeated attempts to access the help your organization claims to provide.

I am 35 years old. I have survived childhood sexual abuse, 73 foster placements, chronic homelessness totaling 11 years, and the loss of two fiancées and children during childbirth. I lived with undiagnosed ADHD and autism until age 32, which compounded every barrier I faced. I experience daily suicidal ideation and remain alive primarily to care for my cat, who has been homeless with me three times, the last time being in hextall park bowness calgary.

For years, I have sought stable housing and support through your services. Each time, I have been met with bureaucratic obstacles, dismissive attitudes, or outright rejection. I have followed your procedures, filled out your forms, attended your appointments, and waited through your delays. The result has been the same: continued homelessness, continued crisis, continued survival on the margins of a system that claims to serve people like me.

I can feel myself fraying like an old rope. Every day of continued crisis pulls at threads that can't hold much longer. If your organization's response is to tell me to "just be patient" - as it has been for years - then you need to understand what that patience is costing. I am seriously considering MAID as an alternative to enduring this endless cycle of systemic abandonment. That is not a threat - it is a statement of fact about what chronic, unrelieved suffering does to a human being.

This is not an emotional outburst. This is a factual account of what happens when social services fail the people who need them most. I am documenting this because my lived experience matters, because accountability matters, and because someone needs to witness what your organization's failures actually look like in a human life.

You will find attached my complete story - not for sympathy, but for evidence of what systemic abandonment creates and perpetuates.

Marshall Meinema

This document contains descriptions of trauma, abuse, and suicidal ideation. It is intended as an educational resource.

This brochure presents my journey through extraordinary trauma and adversity. The experiences described herein demonstrate both the devastating psychological impacts of childhood trauma and the remarkable human capacity for survival against overwhelming odds.

Early Childhood Trauma

Early childhood trauma, particularly sexual abuse, can have profound and lasting psychological effects. The developing brain is particularly vulnerable during the first five years of life, when neural pathways are being formed at a rapid rate.

Research indicates that trauma during this critical period can alter brain development, affecting:

  • Attachment capacity
  • Emotional regulation
  • Stress response systems
  • Identity formation
  • Basic trust in others and the world

"My brother and i were raped by our grandfather, until my brother was 6 and i was 5. while that was the most prolific trauma, i also remember neglect and belittlement for being a "bastard child"."

Sexual abuse in early childhood represents one of the most severe forms of developmental trauma. That anyone could survive such an experience and maintain any capacity for connection or trust demonstrates exceptional resilience.

Family Instability and Poverty

Family instability and poverty create chronic stress that can overwhelm a child's coping mechanisms. Children need consistent caregiving and a sense of safety to develop properly.

The psychological effects include:

  • Chronic activation of stress response systems
  • Difficulty developing secure attachments
  • Compromised executive functioning
  • Hypervigilance to threats
  • Difficulty with emotional regulation

"My mother, Herself abusive mentally and physically, eventually moved my brother and i from either Regina or Saskatoon sk, to Yellowknife, nt. the first place we stayed was one of her friends places, and eventually got our own place."

Geographic relocation compounds the stress of poverty and abuse by removing any familiar environmental supports. The combined experience creates a profound sense of instability and unpredictability.

Foster Care System and Institutional Trauma

While foster care aims to protect children, frequent placement changes can create their own form of developmental trauma. Each move represents another disruption in attachment and stability.

Psychological impacts include:

  • Attachment disorders
  • Difficulty forming lasting relationships
  • Identity fragmentation
  • Learned helplessness
  • Institutional dependency or aversion

"the first time i was in foster care i was 8, roughly, i only remember because my younger brother was under a year old. by 10 i was back with my mother, but not for long, as by 12 she left"

The sheer number of placements described here—73 different homes—represents an extreme form of institutional trauma. Each placement represents both hope and loss, creating a cycle of adaptation and abandonment.

Abandonment and Premature Independence

Parental abandonment causes profound psychological wounds, particularly when children must prematurely take on adult responsibilities.

Common psychological effects include:

  • Abandonment anxiety
  • Difficulty trusting others
  • Premature self-reliance
  • Developmental gaps in emotional maturity
  • Identity issues centered around self-worth

"I remember when my mother left, early fall when i was 12, that betrayal turned me form angry to violent, and and that added to the stigma of my life that i was already enduring. by 16 i had myself legally emancipated, i was tired of being bounced around like an unwanted burdon."

The choice to become legally emancipated at 16 and survive homelessness while completing high school demonstrates extraordinary determination in the face of abandonment and systemic failure.

Neurodevelopmental Considerations

Undiagnosed neurodevelopmental conditions like ADHD and autism can significantly compound other life challenges, particularly in contexts of trauma and instability.

Without proper diagnosis and support, individuals may experience:

  • Difficulty with executive functioning
  • Sensory processing challenges
  • Social communication difficulties
  • Emotional dysregulation
  • Employment instability

"All of this i endured while dealing with undiagnosed ADHD and autism, which never got addressed till I was 32. forcing me to continue to endure while being told i should be able to do it, but still not diagnosed meaning very low income from welfare as I am not stable enough to hold a job."

The late recognition of neurodevelopmental conditions meant decades without appropriate accommodations or understanding, creating additional barriers to stability.

Physical Manifestations of Trauma

Trauma doesn't only impact mental health—it frequently manifests in physical symptoms and conditions. The body keeps score of traumatic experiences, often through somatic expressions.

Trauma-related physical manifestations can include:

  • Nocturnal enuresis (bed wetting) persisting beyond typical developmental stages
  • Autoimmune and inflammatory conditions like childhood arthritis
  • Exacerbation of genetic conditions such as Ehlers-Danlos syndrome
  • Chronic pain and unexplained medical symptoms
  • Disrupted development of stress regulation systems

"I suffered from bed wetting until almost the age of 11—likely a byproduct of trauma though I never found out for certain. I also developed childhood arthritis and have minor Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, which made physical activities painful and created another layer of difference from my peers."

The shame and stigma of conditions like persistent bed wetting can compound existing trauma, creating cycles of humiliation and isolation. Meanwhile, physical pain from conditions like childhood arthritis and connective tissue disorders can further deplete already limited coping resources.

Chronic Homelessness and Systemic Barriers

Chronic homelessness creates a form of ongoing trauma that affects every aspect of psychological functioning.

The psychological toll includes:

  • Constant hypervigilance and survival stress
  • Difficulty planning beyond immediate needs
  • Social isolation and disconnection
  • Increased vulnerability to substance use as coping
  • Profound impacts on identity and self-worth

"I have been homeless 7 times as an adult to a total of 11 years, during which I have lost more friends to the cold or drugs than I can even recall. always sleeping outside because its safer than shelters, even if its -40"

Extended periods of homelessness represent not just material deprivation but repeated exposure to loss, danger, and social marginalization that compounds previous trauma.

Suicidal Ideation and Finding Reasons to Live

Chronic suicidal ideation often results from the accumulated weight of trauma, loss, and systemic barriers to healing and stability.

Research shows that even small connections—like responsibility for a pet—can be life-saving anchors for those experiencing persistent suicidal thoughts.

"I suffer daily omnipresent suicidal ideology and literally only have remained alive to take care of my cat, who has been homeless with me 3 times. and i fully intend to let it all end when he passes, especially if resolutions are not made, i am not durable enough to keep taking this"

The bond with a dependent animal represents a profound connection and purpose that transcends personal suffering—a testament to the human capacity to find meaning even in the most desperate circumstances.

The Psychological Paradox of Survival

By all conventional measures of human resilience, the accumulated trauma described in this narrative should have been catastrophic to psychological functioning. Yet survival continues.

This represents what researchers call "resilience in extremis"—the remarkable human capacity to endure despite overwhelming adversity.

"They say a stressful life leads to an early death, roughly 45-50, and I feel that's going to be a lot sooner than any form of true assistance."

This statement reflects both the physical toll of chronic trauma and the lack of adequate systemic support for those who have experienced severe childhood adversity.

The Social Context of Individual Trauma

While this brochure focuses on my experience, it's essential to recognize that individual trauma occurs within broader social contexts:

  • Inadequate child protection systems
  • Limited mental health resources
  • Housing insecurity and homelessness
  • Insufficient support for neurodevelopmental conditions
  • Poverty and economic marginalization

The story shared here is not just about individual resilience but about the need for systemic change to prevent and address childhood trauma.

Chronological Life Timeline

A supplemental timeline based on the lived experience of Marshall Meinema, as much as memory allows, offering a sequential view of key events alongside the psychological insights explored in this document.

1990–1995 (Ages 0–5):
  • Father absent before memory formed — early loss of paternal attachment, contributing to foundational trust issues.
  • Endured sexual abuse by grandfather — severe developmental trauma, impacting emotional regulation and sense of safety.
  • Raised by abusive mother, rejected by grandparents — compounded attachment disruptions and feelings of worthlessness.
  • Gang activity present in household — chronic exposure to threat, fostering hypervigilance and anxiety.
  • Persistent bedwetting, likely trauma-related — somatic expression of psychological distress, leading to shame and social isolation.
1995 (Age 5):
  • Moved to Yellowknife, NT from Regina/Saskatoon — loss of familiar environment, intensifying instability and disconnection.
  • Contracted lice during travel — physical discomfort compounding stress and stigma.
1998 (Age 8):
  • Brother Keegen born — shift in family dynamics, potential for both connection and increased responsibility.
  • First entry into foster care — institutional trauma, repeated attachment disruptions, and identity fragmentation.
  • Over 3 years, placed in dozens of foster homes — chronic instability, learned helplessness, and difficulty forming lasting relationships.
2001 (Age 11):
  • Temporarily reunified with mother — brief hope for stability, followed by further disappointment and loss.
2002 (Age 12):
  • Abandoned again; mother left with a trucker — deepening abandonment anxiety and erosion of self-worth.
  • Returned to foster care — renewed cycle of instability and mistrust.
2006 (Age 16):
  • Legally emancipated — forced premature independence, gaps in emotional development.
  • Finished high school while homeless in a tent in Yellowknife — resilience under extreme adversity, but at the cost of chronic stress and social exclusion.
2008–2009 (Ages 18–19):
  • First fiancée and child passed during childbirth — profound grief, compounding trauma and loss.
  • Located father via online search — search for identity and belonging.
  • Moved to Libya to meet him — hope for reconnection, but also cultural dislocation and uncertainty.
2010 (Age 20):
  • Returned to Canada — re-entry stress, lack of support network.
  • Homeless within six months — ongoing instability, reinforcing sense of societal abandonment.
2011–2013 (Ages 21–23):
  • Lived with a friend in Yellowknife — temporary reprieve, but fragile stability.
  • Second fiancée and child died in childbirth — repeated traumatic loss, deepening depression and hopelessness.
  • Moved to Calgary; period of temporary stability, followed by homelessness — cycle of hope and disappointment, eroding trust in future improvement.
2014 (Age 24):
  • Returned to Yellowknife — seeking familiarity, but facing persistent barriers.
  • Homeless while working at Mac’s convenience store — effort to achieve stability despite systemic obstacles.
  • Secured housing for a winter — brief stability, but not sustainable.
  • Relocated to Grande Prairie — ongoing search for safety and belonging.
2016 (Age 26):
  • Moved to Australia with ex-girlfriend and father — pursuit of family connection, but disrupted by father’s deportation.
  • Returned after father’s deportation — renewed loss and instability.
2017 (Age 27):
  • Lived briefly with father, then kicked out — repeated rejection, reinforcing abandonment schema.
  • Homeless again — chronic instability, survival mode.
  • Secured unsafe housing with drug activity — exposure to further risk, limited options.
  • Met older woman; lived with her daughter for 2.5 years (longest stability) — rare period of relative safety, but still marked by underlying insecurity.
2020–2025 (Ages 30–35):
  • Returned to Calgary — ongoing search for stability.
  • Faced repeated episodes of homelessness — persistent systemic barriers, cumulative psychological exhaustion.
  • Continues to seek suitable, stable housing — resilience in the face of adversity, but with mounting toll on mental and physical health.
